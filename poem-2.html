The Solitude of
a Wandering Star

It is a curious thing to be told that one’s existence requires a justification, as if the soul were a

ledger that must always find a way to balance its accounts. They call me "introverted," a clinical

term for what is, in truth, a quiet preference for the shadows of one’s own mind over the

blinding glare of the marketplace.


I often wonder if it is "right" to be this way. I walk through the world like a ghost haunting a

house that has long since changed its locks. I am here, yet I do not belong; I speak, yet my

words feel like stones dropped into a well so deep they never hit the bottom. This loneliness—

this sense of being a misplaced gear in a grand machine—is a heavy coat to wear in the heat of midsummer.


Yet, when I turn my gaze toward the heavens, I find a strange, cold comfort. The universe is not

a crowded room; it is an immense, echoing void where the stars are separated by distances

that the human mind cannot even begin to calculate. Does the North Star apologize for its

isolation? Does a nebula weep because it cannot fit into the palm of a hand?


To be "misfit" is, perhaps, to be of a different celestial magnitude. Perhaps I am not a broken

part of this world, but a stray atom from a galaxy that has not yet been named. My gravity is

different. My light travels at a frequency that the eyes of others are not yet tuned to see.


There is a certain quiet dignity in this estrangement. Soseki once suggested that to live is to

endure a constant, subtle friction against the world. If I do not fit, it is because my edges

remain sharp, unweathered by the constant rubbing of the crowd. To fit perfectly is to be a

pebble in a stream, worn smooth and indistinguishable from a thousand others. I would rather

be the jagged meteor, burning alone as it cuts through the atmosphere.


Is it "well" to be as I am? The question itself dissolves when held against the vastness of the

cosmos. We are all, in the end, briefly animated stardust, flickering for a second between two

eternities of darkness. If the universe is composed mostly of silence and space, then my

introversion is not a flaw—it is a reflection of the very fabric of reality.


I am alone, but I am vast. I do not fit, because I am still expanding. And in the quiet of my own

company, I find that even a lonely star is enough to light its own corner of the night.
